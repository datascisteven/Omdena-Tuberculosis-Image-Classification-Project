The treatment of tuberculosis (TB) typically involves a combination of antibiotics taken over several months to effectively eliminate the bacteria causing the infection. Key treatment options include: Isoniazid: An important first-line drug that targets the TB bacteria's growth and replication[^1^][^3^]. Rifampin (Rifampicin): Another first-line drug that works by inhibiting bacterial RNA synthesis[^3^][^6^]. Pyrazinamide: This drug is often used in the initial phase of treatment to rapidly reduce bacterial populations[^1^][^6^]. Ethambutol: Used alongside other drugs to prevent the development of resistance[^3^]. Streptomycin: Typically used in combination with other drugs for severe cases[^7^]. Directly Observed Therapy (DOT): Ensuring patients take their medication under supervision, improving treatment adherence[^4^]. Combination Therapy: TB treatment usually involves a combination of multiple drugs to prevent the development of drug resistance and increase efficacy[^3^]. Long Treatment Duration: TB treatment courses can last for several months, ranging from 6 to 9 months or even longer[^5^][^8^]. It's important to follow the prescribed treatment plan diligently and complete the full course of antibiotics to ensure effective eradication of the bacteria and prevent the development of drug-resistant strains. The choice of treatment regimen may vary based on factors like drug resistance patterns and the presence of other health conditions.
